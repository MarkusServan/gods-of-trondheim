Bethel Trondheim: A Pentecostal Church in a Multicultural Setting

Through fieldwork, interviews, and participation in different services, we explored how Bethel practices faith, community, and Pentecostal tradition in everyday life.

Anna Vilhelmina Breivik, Hans Einar Tuhus Fuglum, Even Fjeldstad, Evan Lien, Rahul Uthayakumar April 22, 2026  ·  8 min read

For this project, our group decided to focus on Bethel Trondheim, one of the Pentecostal churches in the city. We chose Bethel because several of us were curious about how Pentecostalism is practiced in Norway today, and the church seemed like a good example of a congregation with a mix of traditions, cultures, and worship styles. Since Bethel is located in the city center and has an active community, it was a natural place for us to carry out our fieldwork.

During our visits, we noticed that the services and activities at Bethel are shaped by the people who attend them. The congregation includes members from different countries and age groups, and this influences both the atmosphere and the way worship is expressed. Instead of a very formal structure, the gatherings feel open and participatory, with music, prayer, and shared experiences playing a central role.

By studying Bethel, we wanted to understand how a modern Pentecostal church functions in Trondheim, and how faith is practiced in a multicultural setting. Our observations, interviews, and participation in different services gave us insight into how the church organizes its activities, how people engage with worship, and how community is built in everyday church life.

"Kristendommen i Norge i dag er preget av mangfold, med et bredt spekter av menigheter, tradisjoner og uttrykk for tro."

— Helje Kringlebotn Sødal, Norsk kristendomshistorie 1800–2020

Bethels church service

The church service is central to Bethel’s identity, and our fieldwork showed that worship is shaped by both Pentecostal tradition and the cultural diversity within the congregation. Compared to more traditional churches, Bethel’s services place strong emphasis on participation, emotion, and shared experience. This reflects a Pentecostal understanding of worship as expressive and community-oriented rather than formal or reserved.

In the interview, the pastor explained that each of Bethel’s services develops its own atmosphere, shaped by the cultural background of those who attend. He described the Norwegian service as more everyday and relaxed, with people arriving in ordinary clothes and engaging in a down-to-earth style of worship. In contrast, the service attended by many African members is experienced as more celebratory, where people “come dressed up” and treat the gathering almost like a weekly festivity. The Slavic service, held later in the afternoon, has a different tone, again more melancholic and heartfelt, which the pastor described as “inderlig.” These examples illustrate how Bethel adapts its worship to different cultural expressions within the same congregation.

Beyond these cultural differences, the pastor also highlighted how Bethel shapes the service to create a sense of closeness and equality. He emphasized that they aim to reduce the distance between leaders and the congregation, reflecting a belief that all members share equal spiritual value. This is visible in the worship style: instead of a traditional organ, Bethel uses a band, and the services often include clapping, dancing, and active engagement from the congregation.

This emphasis on participation continues after the service as well. Bethel intentionally shortens the worship gathering to make space for fellowship in the cafe afterward, emphasizing that building relationships is understood as an essential part of Christian practice. In this way, worship and social interaction are seen as two connected aspects of the same religious experience.

"There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit distributes them. There are different kinds of service, but the same Lord. There are different kinds of working, but in all of them and in everyone it is the same God at work."

- The Bible, 1 Corinthians 12:4-6 (NIV)

Bethels use and reading of the Bible

The Bible is very important to the Pentecostals. Pastor of the church, Erlend Indregaard, said that he treats the Bible like a set of instructions on how to live life. He quoted from John 1:1-14: The word was with God, the Word was God. And the word became flesh and dwelt among us. Jesus is the human incarnation of God, but also of the word of God, and it is this link to Jesus which makes the Bible important.

There are many ways in which to interpret the Bible. Indregaard says that within the Norwegian Pentecostal church there is a shared theology, but disagreements can occur if there is a difference in what a church chooses to focus on. Some keep old practices whilst others innovate.

Although they have a typically conservative reading of the Bible, they recognise that the Bible was written in a different time. Indregaard says that he wants to find out what the text meant to the people for the time in which it was written. The meaning that those words had to the people living then is what Indregaard tries to anchor his practice of the Bible in.

It is also important to differentiate between the long drawn lines that go through the whole text, and one off passages. Looking more closely at one off passages Indregard himself tries to find out why these are included.

Indregaard placed particular emphasis on the biblical notion of sin. He says that we are all sinful, and that we need to deal with it. If we don’t, it can have negative effects on both ourselves and our surroundings.

The Holy Spirit in Bethel’s Worship

The holy spirit is a recurring subject in pentecostalism. The first occurrence is already in the term pentecost. Which is an event that commemorates the descent of the holy spirit upon the Jesus’ apostles, Mary and other followers of Christ. The pentecostal movement emphasizes direct personal experience of God through baptism with the Holy Spirit.

Pentecostalism can be found in Trondheim in the pentecostal church: Bethel. In our interview with Bethel’s main pastor, Erlend, he talked about how his personal experience with the Holy Spirit led him to pentecostalism. How the spirit appears in the act of speaking in tongue and general charismatics.

Erlend was an adrenaline-seeking young adult with a constant longing for something more. He did not have any encounters with God. When he was 19 years old he met some christian boys at a camp in Oppdal, they offered to pray for him. They began praying and talking in tongues, and it didn’t take long before he felt a strange feeling within. He describes this as the first time that God spoke to him, telling him that “this is a small sample of what you have been longing for”. He immediately knew that this was correct, he had been longing for God’s power – healing, a physical manifestation of the gospel. This encounter with God was what led him to further cultivate his relationship with God.

Erlend described how his eczema was cured through the act of charismatic praying, and how he has helped others through the act. He describes speaking in tongues as the personal language of prayer. When speaking or praying in tongue, the mind or intellect is completely decoupled from the speech. Meaning that it is perceived as a completely non-understandable language. But it is felt as a spiritual language, which is said to take place when you don't know what to pray for. Erlend says that when he prays in tongue, there sometimes comes a picture or words to mind which may help the situation. An example of this is when praying for someone who can't be diagnosed, a picture of where the illness takes place may help.

"All of them were filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit enabled them."

- The Bible, Acts 2:4 (NIV)

“Love Does”: A Seasonal Theme of Action

When we were visiting the church we kept seeing and hearing the phrase “Love does”. We got curious about this, and we initially thought maybe it was a valentines theme or something. It turns out it is something bigger than all that at the pentecostal church Bethel in Tronheim. It seems to be a seasonal theme, and from a facebook reel we found more information about this theme:

“This spring, Bethel’s theme is “Love Does.” 💕 Together, we want to fix our eyes on Jesus – our greatest and most perfect example. In Him, we see how love was not only spoken about, but lived out through action, compassion, service, and self-sacrifice. The theme reminds us that true love is shown in what we do in our encounters with others. We therefore want to challenge one another to follow Jesus’ example, to see the people around us, and to let love take concrete shape in our everyday lives.”
Link to facebook post

In the interview we had with Pastor Erlend at Bethel Tronheim we also talked to him about this theme. We had written the questions before even knowing it was a seasonal theme, but found out through their website and social media that it in fact was a theme for this half-year. From the interview we gathered that the church usually has differing themes for every half of the year. We were also told that the last theme they had before Love does was “Go and make”.

Love coupons - Will you take the challenge? A task in love
Both times we were visiting the church there were these cards on a table right inside the first door you open to go out from the main area. These small cards have the title love coupons, and all have different tasks that you are encouraged to do. I imagine to spread love and kindness, because of how much this means to the church and their faith. Listening to how it is talked about in the church services both from the time we were there physically experiencing it but also from the long videos on youtube you can get a taste of how prominent this theme is in the church right now. It is a well fitting theme to convey and connect through belief.

Decorations inside and outside the church The theme Love does does not stop at the church services and the love coupons, it also feels very prominent both inside and outside the church building itself. At least this is how it was for me as an outsider, who has never been at a pentecostal church. The outside of the building has a sign hanging on it, with the words love does in big text decorating the entrance.

When we were inside the church you could see a big display up on stage that spelled love does, there were also a lot of hearts cut out in red paper hanging from the ceiling. The wall to the right of the stage had hearts hanging on most of the wall in front of rectangular windows, letting the light flow into the room though these hearts.

The love does theme did affect our experience of the church, because of how much of what we observed was in this theme, and therefore it makes sense to present it properly here. The church service, the decorations and the atmosphere were all affected by and presented through this theme.

"Kjærligheten gjør. Det er ikke bare ord, den aktiverer også handling."

— Pastor Erlend Indregaard, Betel Trondheim

School structure and demographics

In the interview with the church pastor Bethel's presence in Trondheim got bigger. Not only do they have a church in the middle of Prinsensgate, home to multiple religious groups, but their church holds three different congregations separated by language.

The church is also closely affiliated to Tomasskolen, the christian primary school in Trondheim for children aged 6 through 15. Tomasskolen first opened in the year 2000, funded by the Bethel church. It currently has 150 pupils, and has room for 100 more according to Wikipedia commons. When looking at the Schools own website it claims only to have 89 pupils. Therefore it is natural to consider the student population to be somewhere between 89 and 150 pupils. A pretty small school, when it is split between 10 grades.

The church also has its own Kulturskole, where children can learn musical instruments and musical theory. Skaperverket Kulturskole was started in 2020 by the Federation of Pentecostal church. There they teach the children instruments, both practical music and musical theory. The pastor told us in the interview we had with him on the 18th of March that the pupils of the Skaperverket School often accompany the church services and happenings with their instruments, and that they see it as a natural stage for their young people to engage with their art.

The pastor also talked about the church's elderly population. Every other week, the church invites them to a Formiddagsmøte, where the elders sing their songs and meet each other. He said that for a lot of these people, that is their only output during a week. The week they (Bethel) don't have the elderly for Formiddagsmøte, many of them go to Salem, which is the other pentecostal church just a few meters up the road from Bethel.

Bethel and Salem cooperate on many fronts, especially within the arts and during big events and festivals. Both congregations have pupils at Tomasskolen.

Conclusion

Our visits to Bethel gave us a clearer picture of how a Pentecostal church works in a local setting. The congregation is shaped by many cultures, and this comes through in the worship, the atmosphere, and the way people take part in the services. What stood out to us was how faith is expressed through practice, through music, prayer, and the different ways people participate.

The pastor’s stories, the focus on the Holy Spirit, and the variety of services all showed how Bethel combines shared beliefs with different cultural expressions. Even though the congregation is diverse, the church manages to create a sense of belonging for the people who attend.

Overall, Bethel reflects a form of Christianity that is lived out in community. It is a place where people gather, contribute, and shape the church together, and this gave us insight into how faith is practiced in Trondheim today.

"So in Christ we, though many, form one body, and each member belongs to all the others."

— The Bible, Romans 12:5 (NIV)
